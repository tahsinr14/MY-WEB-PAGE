There are many things in the world and beyond that we don't know about.
And the space, is the mysterious one. The Solar System is a gravitational bound system
which consists of the Sun, the stars and the objects that orbit around the Sun,
directly or indirectly.
While traveling through space, asteroids sometimes collide with each other and break up into smaller pieces.
Comets shed dust as they roam the solar system. These 'break ups' result in numerous small particles
and fragments, called meteoroids, which orbit the sun.



Most of the meteoroids are small and rocky. When one approaches the Earth
at a high speed, it burns up as it goes through Earth's atmosphere. From this, a meteor or a "shooting star" is born.

There are also fireballs which are larger meteoroids and they make very impressive sky displays as
they break into particles and burn up in their passage through Earth's atmosphere.
Some meteoroids survive passage through Earth's atmosphere and hit the ground. These are called meteorites.



Meteoroids are objects in space that range in size from dust grains to small asteroids. Imagine them as “space rocks".
When a meteoroid survives a trip through the atmosphere and hits the ground, it’s called a meteorite.

We all know the famous Halley's Comet which returns to Earth's vicinity about every 75 years.
The last time it was here was in 1986, and it is projected to return in 2061.

Comets are rather small, fragile, uneven shaped bodies and, like asteroids, they are left
over from the solar system development process. Comets, however, are icy dirtballs that form in the outer
solar system. The icy surface is submerged with dust, stones and particles from space.


The most distant comets may take more than 30 million years to complete one orbit.
And comets with smaller orbital paths can take
less than 200 years to orbit the sun, making them more anticipated.

When comets are far from the sun, they are usually very cold, icy dirtballs. However, when they approach the sun,
their surfaces begin to warm and explosive materials vaporize. These vaporizing gases carry
small dust grains with them, which form an atmosphere of gas and dust and can look like a
bright tail when seen from Earth.


Comets usually have highly eccentric elliptical orbits, and they have a wide range of orbital periods,
ranging from several years to potentially several millions of years.
The appearance of a comet is called an apparition. According to astronomer Gerard Kuiper in 1951,
there are a disc-like belt of icy bodies exists beyond Neptune.
These icy objects, occasionally pushed by gravity into orbits bringing them closer to the Sun,
become the so-called short-period comets. There are long-period comets also.
Most comets travel a safe distance from the Sun―comet Halley comes no closer than 89 million kilometers (55 million miles).
However, some comets, called sungrazers, crash straight into the Sun or get so close that they break up and evaporate.



Asteroids are minor planets, especially of the inner Solar System.
They are rocky pieces left over from the formation of the solar system about 4.6 billion years ago.
Most asteroids orbit the sun in a belt between Mars and Jupiter. Scientists think there are probably
millions of asteroids, ranging widely in size from hundreds of kilometers across to less than one kilometer
(a little more than one-half mile) wide.

Asteroids are somewhat different from comets and meteoroids. In the case of comets, the difference
is in anatomy: as asteroids are mainly consists of mineral and rock, comets on the other hand are primarily consists of dust and ice.
Moreover, asteroids formed closer to the sun, preventing the development of cometary ice. The difference between asteroids and
meteoroids is mainly of size: meteoroids have a diameter of one meter or less, whereas asteroids have a diameter of greater than
one meter. Also, meteoroids can be composed of either cometary or asteroidal materials.
